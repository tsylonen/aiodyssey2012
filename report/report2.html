AI Odyssey 2012

Challenge 2: Repeated Prisoner's Challenge

The topic of challenge number two was repeated prisoner's dilemma, a version of the well known prisoner's dilemma in which the game is played a number of times against the same opponent and the prison years are summed up.

In this challenge there are 8 opposing AI of which all but two use a known strategy. One game is 100 rounds long and the scores against all the bots are summed up in the end.

An optimal score in this challenge could easily be attained by figuring out a short move sequence that would trigger unique responses from each of the opposing AI players and select the optimal strategy against the detected AI. I decided against doing this as it would not have been a very interesting approach and there was to be a tournament of student AIs playing against each other in the end in which the strategy would probably have failed very badly.

I started out by implementing a the well known tit-for-tat player which starts off by cooperating, then playing the opponents previous move. Tit-for-tat is known to be one of the overall best strategies for repeated prisoner's dilemma and it's very easy to implement. This fared pretty well against many of the opposing strategies but got a score that few hundred over the required minimum.

As I was not very likely to find a general strategy that would fare well enough against the given set of opponents I decided to add a few tweaks that would target the given opponents without altering the basic tit-for-tat mechanism too much.

I added a second round of cooperation at the beginning of each match. This avoided going into a loop of always making the worst decision against the tit-for-tat which starts off by defecting. Next I added simple methods for detecting if the opponent always cooperates, always chooses the opposing move my AI did on the last round and playing randomly. Against these AIs my bot would simply defect on each round. The final tweak I made was always defecting on the last round.

With these straightforward rules my AI was able to get a decent score and still play pretty close to the normal tit-for-tat rules against most opponents. It would not have been hard to reverse engineer the secret strategies and making methods to try and detect them and choose an optimal counter strategy but I decided it wouldn't be very interesting and didn't to that.
